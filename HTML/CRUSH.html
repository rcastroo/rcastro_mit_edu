Summary
I was Team captain, Drive Team Captain, and Head Programmer for FIRST Robotics Competition team CRUSH 1011. I was on the team from December 2015 until December 2017. The team was very successful, and contributed a big part to my learning. In 2017, we won the FRC Robotics World Championship, where hundreds of the best teams in the world compete as the 1st pick. Most of my knowledge about robotics has been self-taught and expressed through programs like these. At CRUSH, I created several control schemes and routines for the robot to be capable of performing autonomous feats that no other team in the world could. Additionally, I worked on several critical projects in Business, Media, Outreach, Design, and Fabrication.

Programming/Controls
As Head Programmer of the team without a Programming mentor, many projects took time to develop, but in the end we ended up with consistent autonomous routines that were unique and contributed heavily to our success. Personally, I worked hundreds of hours on perfecting our code to work as intended.
We used these concepts:
  • OpenCV based vision tracking
  • Path generation and following
  • Arm PID angle control
  • Collision detection
  • Independent controlling systems for individual robot components
  • Modular code meant to be reused year past year
These concepts can be best seen with one of our autonomous routines: the 3 gear. Here, the robot is running completely on its own with only encoders, a gyroscope,a camera, current sensors, and precise coding & debugging to find itself and score on a 1" wide peg.
Design
I worked on critical robot components that had to withstand other 140lb robots crashing into them full speed.
Here are two of the projects I worked on:

2017 Collector
This is a double jointed, clamping mechanism to pick up 11" plastic gears. You can see it in action in the previous gif above. The bottom plate is 0.065" lexan, which scrapes against the ground at about a 5° angle, which is just right to allow the gear to slide in as you drive into it. After it's inside of the collector, the clamping cylinder actuates, pressing a bar with foam on top of the gear onto the bottom plate, holding it tight. Then, the wrist, which is actuated by a pneumatic cylinder, snaps back up to a "scoring" position which brings the collector away from the ground. Additionally, the arm can rotate a full 90° to allow the whole mechanism to be tucked into the robot into a "carrying" position which prevents collector damage from robot and field hits and allows the whole robot to fit into the alloted size.

2016 Grappling Hook
This was a really fun one. For the 2016 season, there was an optional challenge, where a robot could reach onto a 1" bar over 6 feet above the ground, and then had to pull itself up a few feet into the air. Our team decided not to focus on that challenge, as it was a relatively low amount of points. We instead focused more on the more pressing challenges that could make more of a difference. Our ability to analyze how to divert our team's energy is what allowed us to be really successful compared to other teams with similar resources.
Towards the end of the season, however, our main parts were solidified, and we had some time to spare, so we decided to tackle the challenge, and I spearheaded the design. Since we had <5 lbs to work with, most designs were out of the question, and we had to come up with something creative. We had seen a few other teams succeed with a grappling hook type mechanism that launched a hook onto the bar and then winched the robot up. A local team had a design that was very attractive, so we looked at it to try to approximate it, however, there were some significant differences between our robots which made our version need to have much more functionality. Instead, we revamped most of the parts and it led to this: Since our robot was built into a "short" configuration, our launcher had to actuate up from a horizontal position onto an angle which would hit the bar. Afterwards, the launcher had to release control of the hook, allowing the surgical tubing launch the hook. Once the hook latched onto the bar, a winch would real in kevlar rope attached to the hook. Easy right? Well... we only had weight for 1 more motor. So all three of these actions had to be manipulated by just that one motor. To allow the launcher to actuate upwards to a vertical shooting position, we created a ripping mechanism, where once the motor reaches a certain length, it would snap a replaceable cord which allowed the launcher to be pulled back by a spring. Afterwards, a similar snapping mechanism would release a grabber that held onto the hook, allowing the hook to shoot up. Then the excess kevlar rope was reeled in to bring the robot up. All with one motor.

We did not end up using it in a match in competition due to time constraints, however, it successfully worked in our shop. It was simply an amazing, fun project to work with that had tight design constraints.

Business
FRC teams are expensive, you have travel costs, robot costs, shop costs, and more. Our budget ran around $50,000 yearly, and somehow we had to find funding for most of it in order to make the cost to the students the least.
I worked on a sponsorship package document, which outlined companies' incentive to sponsor us. Additionally, I went on a few presentations to sponsors, and media outlets, which contributed to tens of thousands of dollars of fundraising to our team.
